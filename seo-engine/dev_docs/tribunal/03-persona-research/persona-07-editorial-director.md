# Persona 07: James Okafor — Editorial Director

---

## 1. Who I Am

My name is James Okafor. I'm the Editorial Director at Meridian SaaS, a B2B software company that sells project management tools to mid-market enterprises. I've been in content and editorial roles for 14 years — started as a journalist, moved into content marketing, and for the last 6 years I've led editorial teams at SaaS companies.

My team is 5 people: 3 content writers, 1 content editor, and 1 content coordinator who manages the publishing calendar and production workflow. I report to the CMO. My technical level is intermediate — I'm comfortable in WordPress, Google Analytics, and content management systems, but I don't write code. I think in narratives, brand positioning, and audience needs, not in APIs and data models.

We produce 12-15 articles per month, plus whitepapers, case studies, and the occasional executive thought leadership piece. Our content drives about 40% of our marketing-qualified leads through organic search and nurture email sequences. Content quality isn't optional for us — it's the engine of our pipeline.

---

## 2. My Daily Workflow

My morning starts at 8 AM reviewing the editorial calendar in Asana. I check what's due this week, what's in draft, what's in review, and what's scheduled for publication. I flag anything that's falling behind and message the relevant writer.

By 9 AM I'm doing editorial reviews. This is the core of my day. I read every piece of content my team produces before it goes to the editor. I'm checking for three things: factual accuracy, strategic alignment with our content pillars, and voice consistency. The voice piece is where I spend most of my energy.

We have a 15-page brand voice style guide that covers tone (authoritative but approachable), vocabulary preferences (we say "team members" not "employees," we say "streamline" not "optimize"), sentence structure guidelines (short paragraphs, active voice, second person), and formatting standards (H2 every 200-300 words, bulleted lists for features, numbered lists for steps).

The problem is that my three writers all have different natural writing voices. Writer A is academic and long-winded. Writer B is punchy and casual — sometimes too casual for B2B. Writer C is solid but generic — their content could belong to any SaaS company. I spend 2-3 hours per day editing drafts to make them sound like they all came from the same editorial voice. That's my biggest time expenditure.

Mid-day is strategy work — planning upcoming content themes, reviewing keyword research from our SEO consultant, aligning content with product launches and campaigns, and meeting with the sales team to understand what questions prospects are asking.

Afternoon is process management — running weekly editorial standups, providing feedback on outlines before writers start drafting, reviewing final edits before publication, and analyzing content performance in GA4 and HubSpot to understand which pieces are driving conversions.

---

## 3. My Top 5 Pain Points

**Pain Point 1: Voice consistency is my full-time job.** I spend 12-15 hours per week editing drafts for voice consistency. Despite the 15-page style guide, every writer interprets it differently. Writer A follows the vocabulary rules but ignores the sentence structure guidelines. Writer B nails the tone but uses forbidden jargon. It's a never-ending editing cycle that doesn't scale. If we hired a fourth writer — which we need to — my editing burden would become unmanageable.

**Pain Point 2: Style guides are ignored because they're unenforceable.** A PDF style guide is a reference document, not an enforcement mechanism. Writers read it once during onboarding and then gradually drift. I do quarterly "voice calibration" sessions where we review recent articles against the guide, but the corrections don't stick. What I need is something that checks voice compliance in real-time, during the writing process, not after the fact.

**Pain Point 3: AI content sounds like AI content.** I've tested ChatGPT, Jasper, Copy.ai, and Writer for B2B content generation. They all produce technically correct content that sounds like it was written by a committee of no one. It's bland, it uses the same transitions ("In today's fast-paced business landscape..."), and it lacks the specific perspective that makes our brand content distinctive. My team can spot AI-generated content in two sentences. If our readers can too, we've damaged our brand.

**Pain Point 4: Onboarding new writers takes months.** When I hire a new writer, it takes 3-4 months before their content consistently matches our voice. That's 3-4 months of heavy editing and revision cycles. If I could give a new writer a tool that guides them toward our voice in real-time, that onboarding period could shrink dramatically.

**Pain Point 5: No feedback loop between performance and voice.** I have a gut feeling that certain voice characteristics correlate with higher engagement — articles with more direct address ("you") and shorter paragraphs tend to get more time-on-page. But I have no data to back this up because there's no way to systematically analyze voice attributes and correlate them with performance metrics.

---

## 4. My Deal-Breakers

- **Real voice cloning, not templates**: The system must analyze our existing published content and learn our specific voice characteristics — not just "professional" or "casual" presets. I need it to understand that we use em dashes frequently, that we open articles with a provocative question, that we never use first person singular.
- **Writer-facing enforcement**: Voice guidance must appear in the writing interface, in real-time, as writers draft. Post-draft voice scoring is too late — I need it to shape the writing as it happens.
- **Granular voice dimensions**: I need to control individual voice attributes independently — formality level, technical depth, sentence length distribution, vocabulary preferences, perspective (first/second/third person), and rhetorical patterns.
- **Content that passes the "author test"**: If I put a ChainIQ-assisted article next to a purely human-written article from my team, I should not be able to tell which is which. That's the quality bar.
- **Integration with our editorial workflow**: It must work within WordPress or Google Docs — where my writers actually write. A standalone editor that requires copy-paste is a workflow regression.

3. **As an editorial director**, I want to see a voice consistency score for every article before publication, with a detailed breakdown showing which voice dimensions (tone, vocabulary, structure, perspective) are aligned and which are drifting, so that I can make data-driven editorial decisions instead of relying on subjective judgment.

4. **As an editorial director**, I want to create multiple voice sub-profiles (blog posts, whitepapers, case studies, thought leadership) under our master brand voice, so that the system understands that a case study should be more formal and data-heavy while a blog post can be more conversational, while both still sound like Meridian.

5. **As an editorial director**, I want voice-performance correlation reports that show me which voice attributes (sentence length, direct address frequency, technical depth) are statistically associated with higher engagement metrics, so that I can evolve our voice guide based on what actually resonates with our audience.

## 10. Red Flags

- **Generic voice presets**: If voice intelligence is just choosing between "professional," "casual," and "technical," it's a toy. I need bespoke voice modeling trained on our specific content.
- **Voice drift over time**: If the system's voice output gradually changes without retraining — because the underlying LLM was updated, for example — that's a silent quality degradation I might not catch until readers notice.
- **No human override**: If the system enforces voice rules so rigidly that writers can't make creative choices (an intentionally short fragment for emphasis, a deliberate jargon term in a technical piece), it becomes a constraint rather than a tool.
- **Black-box voice scoring**: If I can't understand why an article scored 72/100 on voice consistency — which specific sentences failed, which voice dimensions are misaligned — the score is meaningless.
- **Content that sounds AI-generated**: If anyone on my team or in my audience can identify ChainIQ-assisted content as machine-generated, the tool has failed its core promise. This includes tells like repetitive transitions, unnaturally perfect grammar, and hedging language patterns common in LLM output.
- **No editorial workflow integration**: If writers have to leave their writing environment to use voice features, adoption will be zero. My writers will not use a separate tool. It must meet them where they work.
