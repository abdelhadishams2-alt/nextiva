# Persona 02: SEO Agency Director

## Marcus Chen — SEO Director, Orbit Digital (Mid-Size Agency)

---

## 1. Who I Am

I am Marcus Chen, SEO Director at Orbit Digital, a mid-size digital marketing agency based in Austin, Texas. I have been in SEO for 11 years — 4 as a freelance consultant, 3 in-house at an e-commerce company, and the last 4 running the SEO practice at Orbit. My team is 8 people: 3 SEO strategists, 2 content writers, 1 technical SEO specialist, 1 link building specialist, and 1 reporting analyst.

We manage 15 active client blogs across verticals: SaaS (4 clients), e-commerce (3), professional services (4), healthcare (2), and fintech (2). Each client has different goals, different audiences, different competitive landscapes, and different brand voices. My technical level is high — I am comfortable with APIs, Google Sheets scripting, basic Python, Screaming Frog, and I have built custom reporting dashboards in Looker Studio. But my time for technical work has shrunk to near zero because I am drowning in client management and reporting.

I bill clients between $3K-8K/month for SEO retainers, and content strategy is the core of what we deliver. My agency's reputation depends on producing measurable organic traffic growth for every client, every quarter.

---

## 2. My Daily Workflow

**7:00 AM** — I open my laptop and immediately check rankings for all 15 clients. I use Semrush for 9 clients and Ahrefs for 6 (because some clients had existing subscriptions they insisted we use). This means I am maintaining two separate tool ecosystems, with different keyword tracking setups, different reporting formats, and different data refresh schedules.

**7:30 AM** — I review Google Search Console for any client that had a significant traffic change. I have alerts set up, but they are noisy and I still manually verify each one. I cross-reference with GA4 to understand if traffic changes are real or measurement artifacts.

**8:00-9:00 AM** — Team standup. Each strategist presents their client updates. We discuss which clients need new content briefs, which need existing content refreshed, and which need technical fixes. Most of the discussion is about content — what to write next, what is underperforming, and why.

**9:00-12:00 PM** — This is supposed to be strategy time, but in reality I spend most of it on reporting. I am pulling data from Semrush, Ahrefs, GSC, and GA4 into Google Sheets, then formatting it into client-specific slide decks. Every client wants a different format. Some want traffic charts. Some want keyword ranking tables. Some want revenue attribution. I have tried to templatize this, but every client is different enough that templates break. My reporting analyst helps, but she is at capacity.

**12:00-1:00 PM** — Client calls. I do 2-3 per day. Most of the call is me presenting the reports I just spent the morning building, answering questions about why a particular article did or did not rank, and defending our content strategy recommendations.

**1:00-3:00 PM** — Content brief creation. For each client, I need to identify the next best topic to write about, research the SERP, analyze competitor content, identify content gaps, define target keywords, outline the article structure, specify the word count and internal linking strategy, and document the brand voice guidelines. Each brief takes 45-90 minutes. I need to produce 3-5 briefs per day across clients to keep the pipeline full.

**3:00-4:00 PM** — Content review. Our writers submit drafts and I review them against the briefs. I check keyword usage, heading structure, content depth, internal links, and voice consistency. I often have to send pieces back for revision because the writer did not have enough context about the client's brand voice or product positioning.

**4:00-5:00 PM** — Publishing and CMS management. 12 of our 15 clients are on WordPress, 2 on Webflow, 1 on a custom CMS. I or my team log into each client's CMS separately to publish content, set meta tags, configure internal links, and add images. There is no unified workflow.

**5:00-6:00 PM** — Admin, invoicing, and catching up on everything I could not get to. I usually work until 7 or 8 PM.

## 3. My Top 5 Pain Points

1. **Reporting consumes 60% of my time.** I did not start an SEO career to become a reporting analyst. I spend more time formatting data into client presentations than I do on actual strategy. Every hour I spend on a report is an hour I am not spending on growing my clients' traffic. The data exists — in GSC, GA4, Semrush, Ahrefs — but unifying it per client, tracking it over time, and presenting it in a client-friendly format is an enormous manual effort.

2. **Per-client content intelligence does not exist.** Every content tool I have tried gives me generic keyword research. I need intelligence that understands Client A is a B2B SaaS company targeting CTOs and Client B is a DTC skincare brand targeting millennials. The same keyword — say, "scalability" — means completely different things for these clients. I need content recommendations that are contextualized to each client's vertical, audience, competitive set, and existing content library.

3. **Brand voice management across 15 clients is impossible.** Each client has a different tone, vocabulary, and style. My writers cannot keep 15 different brand voices in their heads. We have brand voice documents, but they are static PDFs that no one references after onboarding. The result: content that sounds generic, and clients who complain that the blog "does not sound like us."

4. **Tool fragmentation is killing my efficiency.** Semrush for some clients, Ahrefs for others, GSC for all, GA4 for all, Google Docs for briefs, WordPress/Webflow/custom CMS for publishing, Google Sheets for tracking, Google Slides for reporting. I have 15+ browser tabs open at all times. There is no single source of truth for any client.

5. **Content refresh identification is manual and slow.** I know that updating existing content is often higher-ROI than creating new content. But identifying which articles need refreshing, what specifically needs to change, and prioritizing refreshes against new content — this analysis takes hours per client and I only get to it quarterly, when it should be continuous.

---

## 4. My Deal-Breakers

- **Multi-client architecture with proper isolation.** I need to manage 15+ clients in one platform, each with their own data connections, content libraries, voice profiles, and reporting. If ChainIQ is designed for single-site use and I need separate accounts per client, it is not an agency tool.

- **Per-client content intelligence.** The recommendations must be contextual to each client's vertical, audience, and competitive landscape. Generic keyword suggestions that I then have to manually filter and contextualize per client save me nothing.

- **White-label or client-facing reporting.** My clients need to see progress. If I cannot generate client-ready reports (or give clients read-only dashboard access branded to my agency), I am still building reports manually, which defeats the purpose.

- **Integration with both Semrush AND Ahrefs.** I cannot force clients to standardize on one tool. ChainIQ must ingest data from both, plus GSC and GA4, per client.

- **WordPress publishing at minimum.** 80% of my clients are on WordPress. If ChainIQ cannot publish directly to WordPress with proper meta tags, schema markup, and internal linking, it is not saving me publishing time.

## 6. My Wish List

1. **Given** I am onboarding a new client, **When** I connect their GSC, GA4, and Semrush accounts, **Then** I expect ChainIQ to automatically build a content intelligence profile: their existing content inventory, keyword positions, content gaps versus top 5 competitors, and a prioritized list of content opportunities — within 24 hours, not after weeks of manual analysis.

2. **Given** I am managing 15 clients, **When** I open the agency dashboard, **Then** I expect to see a unified view showing each client's content pipeline status (briefs in progress, drafts under review, published, performing, underperforming) with the ability to drill into any client without switching accounts or contexts.

3. **Given** a client asks "what should we write next month?", **When** I open their content intelligence tab, **Then** I expect a ranked list of topic recommendations that accounts for their existing content (to avoid cannibalization), their keyword gaps versus competitors, seasonal trends, and their specific business objectives — not a generic keyword list I have to interpret.

4. **Given** I need to produce a monthly performance report for a client, **When** I click "Generate Report," **Then** I expect a white-labeled PDF or interactive dashboard that shows organic traffic trends, keyword ranking changes, content performance by article, and ROI metrics — formatted professionally without me touching Google Slides.

5. **Given** a client's article was published 6 months ago and has declined in rankings, **When** the feedback loop detects this, **Then** I expect an automatic alert with a specific refresh recommendation: which sections are outdated, which new subtopics to add, which competitor content has outranked it and why.

## 7. Competitor Envy

I am deeply envious of MarketMuse's content inventory and competitive gap analysis. The way MarketMuse can map an entire site's topical authority, identify content gaps at the topic cluster level, and prioritize what to write based on competitive advantage — that is what I want. But MarketMuse is expensive, does not integrate well with my other tools, and is not designed for multi-client agency workflows. It is also purely a planning tool — it does not help with generation, publishing, or performance tracking.

I also envy Surfer SEO's real-time content editor that shows writers exactly how to optimize while they write. If ChainIQ combined MarketMuse's strategic intelligence with Surfer's in-editor guidance, per client, at agency scale — that would be the product I have been looking for.

Frase.io's brief generation speed is also impressive — how it can analyze SERPs and produce a content brief in minutes rather than the hour it takes me manually. But Frase is single-site focused and has no agency layer.

## 10. Red Flags

- **Single-site architecture pretending to be multi-client.** If "multi-client" means "create separate accounts and switch between them," that is not an agency tool. I need true multi-tenant architecture with unified billing, cross-client views, and role-based access so my strategists only see their assigned clients.

- **Slow data ingestion.** If connecting a new client's GSC and GA4 takes days or requires manual CSV uploads, the onboarding cost is too high. I need API-based, automated ingestion that is current within 24 hours.

- **Generic content recommendations.** If the content intelligence layer recommends the same topics to my SaaS client and my skincare client because they both target the keyword "growth," the system does not understand context and I cannot trust it.

- **No audit trail.** My clients need to know what was published, when, and what the performance is. If ChainIQ does not maintain a clear history of content actions, recommendations, and results, I cannot use it in a client-facing context.

- **Pricing that does not scale with clients.** If the pricing model is per-site and I am paying $3K per client, my total cost exceeds $45K/month and the economics do not work for a mid-size agency. I need agency pricing that makes the per-client cost manageable as I grow.

- **Lock-in without data portability.** If I cannot export my clients' data, content libraries, and performance history when a client churns or if I decide to leave ChainIQ, I will not commit my agency's workflow to the platform.
